# <ins>Part 1</ins>

## a) Problem Statement

I am designing a 2D lifting mechanism inside a **150 cm × 50 cm** design space.
The objective is to use a linear actuator and three pin supports to lift a weight
as high as possible while staying within geometric and support constraints.

### <ins>Constraints</ins>
- Design space: **150 cm long, 50 cm tall**
- Exactly **three pin supports** (two on the ground)
- Use **one linear actuator** from the course catalog (fixed stroke length and force)
- All supports, links, and actuator treated as rigid
- Mechanism must operate without interference

### <ins>Objectives</ins>
- Maximize the height the weight can be lifted
- Keep mechanism simple and realistic
- Use the actuator effectively (good leverage and angles)

### <ins>Degrees of Freedom</ins>
The mechanism has **1 degree of freedom** — the actuator extension.  
Changing actuator length rotates the bar and raises the weight.

---

## b) Static Analysis

Using the law of cosines for the actuator–link geometry:

- cos(θ₁) = 0.942 → sin(θ₁) ≈ 0.334  
- cos(θ₂) = 0.497 → sin(θ₂) ≈ 0.866  

Vertical height change:

\[
\Delta h = L(\sin\theta_2 - \sin\theta_1)
\]

With **L = 50 cm**:

\[
\Delta h = 50(0.866 - 0.334) = 84.1\ \text{cm}
\]

Total reachable height:

\[
h_{\max} = 50 + 84.1 = \mathbf{134.1\ cm}
\]

---

### <ins>Force Analysis (Extended Position)</ins>

Actuator force: **36 kN**, applied at ~**60°** to the bar.

Resolve into vertical direction:

\[
W = \frac{36}{\cos 60^\circ} = \frac{36}{0.5} = \mathbf{72\ kN}
\]

Thus, the mechanism can lift **up to 72 kN** assuming rigid links.

# <ins>Part 2</ins>

## a) Maximum Beam Deflection

Now the bar is treated as a **flexible beam** rather than rigid.

---

### <ins>Geometry & Loads</ins>

- Beam length:  
  \[
  L = 50\sqrt{10}\text{ cm} \approx 1.58\ \text{m}
  \]

- Actuator attachment point (midpoint):  
  \[
  a = L/2 = 0.79\ \text{m}
  \]

- Material: **Structural steel**, \(E = 200\text{ GPa}\)

- Initial cross-section guess: **100 mm × 50 mm rectangular bar**

- Second moment of area:  
  \[
  I = \frac{bh^3}{12} = 4.17 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{m}^4
  \]

- Vertical loads:  
  - Tip load: **40 kN**  
  - Actuator vertical force at midpoint: **36 kN**

---

### <ins>Beam Deflection Formulas</ins> (Simply Supported)

1. **Tip load**  
\[
\delta_{\text{tip}} = \frac{P_{\text{tip}}L^3}{3EI}
\]

2. **Midpoint load**  
\[
\delta_{\text{act}} = \frac{P_{\text{act}}a^2(3L - a)}{6EI}
\]

---

### <ins>Numerical Results</ins>

- Tip deflection:  
  **54 mm**

- Actuator midpoint deflection:  
  **25 mm**

- **Total deflection:**  
\[
\delta_{\text{total}} = 80\ \text{mm}
\]

---

## b) Beam Redesign Using W-Shapes

Allowable deflection:

\[
0.02L = 0.02 \times 1.58 = 32\ \text{mm}
\]

Current deflection: **80 mm** → **Too flexible**

Required stiffness increase:

\[
\frac{80}{32} \approx 2.5\times
\]

Thus the required moment of inertia:

\[
I_{\text{req}} = 2.5 \times I_{\text{current}}
= 1.04 \times 10^{-5}\ \text{m}^4
\]

---

### <ins>Choosing a Standard W-Shape</ins>

From AISC Table:

| Beam | \(I_x\) (in⁴) | \(I_x\) (m⁴) |
|------|--------------|--------------|
| **W8×21** | 75.3 | \(3.13 \times 10^{-5}\) |

This beam *exceeds* the required stiffness.

---

### Updated Deflection Check

\[
\delta_{\text{new}}
= 80\text{ mm} \left(\frac{I_{\text{old}}}{I_{\text{new}}}\right)
= 80 \times \frac{4.17e{-6}}{3.13e{-5}}
\approx \mathbf{10.7\ mm}
\]

This is **well below 32 mm**.

---

### Why W8×21 Is Optimal

- High bending stiffness  
- Moderate mass (21 lb/ft)  
- Much stronger than rectangular solid  
- Easy to fabricate and widely used  

**Conclusion:**  
The **W8×21** section is the lightest and most efficient beam that meets the deflection requirement.

### W8×21 Section Properties

- Depth: **8.28 in**  
- Flange width: **5.27 in**  
- Flange thickness: **0.40 in**  
- Web thickness: **0.25 in**  
- Moment of inertia: \(I_x = 75.3 \text{ in}^4\)

This section integrates directly into the mechanism without requiring changes to support or actuator locations.
